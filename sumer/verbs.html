Sumerian Verbs

Sumerian verbs are modified by a series of affixes, the standard sequence is:

Modal Prefix - Conjugation Prefix - Dimensional Prefixes - Pronominal Prefix - Root - Pronomial Suffix

Root

The root of the verb is a usually single syllable, such as du3 "build". However, there are two different types of roots, called hamtu and maru.

The hamtu roughly corresponds to completed actions or unchanging states, while the maru indicates actions that have not yet completed.

For most verbs, the same root can be used in hamtu and maru (e.g. du3 "build").

For some verbs, the maru root is a duplicated version of the hamtu root (e.g. zu hamtu, zu-zu maru "know").

For a few verbs, the maru root is completely different from the hamtu root (e.g. gin hamtu, du maru "go").

A few verbs even use different roots when the subject is singular or plural

Pronominal Prefixes and Suffixes

The pronominal prefixes and suffixes specify the subject and object of the sentence, but which ones are used depends on the nature of the root.

For Intransitive hamtu and maru verbs, add a pronominal suffix from set A to indicate the subject.
Examples: mu.gin.en "I went" (or "you went"), mu.gin.ø "he went", mu.du.ø "he goes".

For Transitive hamtu verbs, add a pronominal prefix to indicate the subject, and a pronominal suffix from set A to indicate either the object, or if the subject is plural.
Examples: mu.n.du3.ø "He built it", immi.n.tud.en "She gave birth to me" (or "to you")

For Ttransitive maru verbs, add a pronominal prefix to indicate the object (maybe), and a pronominal suffix from set B to indicate the subject.
Examples: i3.(b?).du3.en "I am building it " (or "you are building it") i3.(b?).sar3.eš "He is writing it "

Dimensional Prefixes

The dimensional prefixes are added in sequence for any other noun phrases in the sentence besides the subject and object.

Examples: mu.na.n.du3.ø "He built it for him", mu.na.ni.n.du3.ø "He built it for him in there"

Sumerian Dimensional Prefixes

Prefix Case Meaning
-na- dative "to, for"
-ta- ablative-instrumental "from, by"
-da- commitative "with"
-ni- locative "in"
-e- locative-terminative "to, by"
-ši- terminative "towards"

Conjugation Prefixes

The conjugation prefixes have very uncertain meaning, and are often untranslated

The most common ones are -mu-, -ba-, -imma- and a vowel (either -a- or -i-)

According to C. Woods (2009), these conjugation mark "perspective"
-i/a- is neutral, does not mark a particular perspective
-mu- marks an "active" perspective mu-du3 "build", mu-uš2 "kill"
-ba- marks an "medio-passive" perspective, ba-du3 "be built", ba-uš2 "die"
-imma- may mark a "middle" perspectice

-mu- often is used for imperfect, transitive verbs that are the central action, while -imma- and -ba- are used with intransitive, perfect verbs that describe background information

Modal Prefixes

The modal prefixes indicate the "mood" of the verb.

Fun with ambiguities, from "The advice of a supervisor to a younger scribe" ETSCL 5.1.3, line 18
ud na-ab-zal-e-en ji6 na-ab-sed4-e-en
ud na.b.zal.en ji na.b.sed.en
ud="day", zal="pass, shine", ji= "night", sed="be cold"
if the verbs are in maru, then the na- is precative and these clauses read: "Do not pass the day, Do not be cold at night"
if the verbs are in hamtu, then the na- is affirmative and these clauses could read: "Indeed the day shined for me, the night was cold for me"
